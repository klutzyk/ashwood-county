# Ashwood County: Creative Vision

## Working Title

Ashwood County is a placeholder title.

The project, repository, C# namespace, and Godot project name may be renamed once the game develops a clearer identity.

## High-Level Concept

Ashwood County is a top-down 2D zombie-survival RPG about surviving inside a collapsing community.

The player explores a handcrafted county, gathers essential supplies, encounters survivors, manages risk, and makes decisions that alter relationships and future events.

The game's identity should come from the interaction between:

- Survival pressure
- Exploration
- Believable characters
- Difficult choices
- Persistent consequences
- Mystery
- Emergent stories

## Core Fantasy

The player should feel like they are trying to keep themselves and a fragile group of people alive in a place that is steadily becoming more dangerous.

Success should never feel completely secure.

Every expedition should involve questions such as:

- What do we need most?
- Who should come with me?
- Is this rumour trustworthy?
- Is this stranger safe?
- Is the reward worth the risk?
- Who gets the limited medicine?
- Can I afford to help someone else?
- What consequence will this decision create later?

## Design Pillars

### Survival Through Meaningful Choices

Resources should matter because they force decisions, not because the game contains dozens of meters.

Food, medicine, safety, time, and trust should create pressure that the player can understand.

### The Survivors Create the Story

Named survivors should have:

- Recognisable personalities
- Personal goals
- Relationships
- Opinions
- Important memories
- Reasons for their behaviour

Characters should not exist only to distribute quests.

### A World Worth Remembering

The world should be handcrafted enough that players remember locations.

A player should be able to think:

> This is the pharmacy where we found the antibiotics.

or:

> This is the farmhouse where everything went wrong.

The initial world should be compact, dense, and purposeful rather than enormous and repetitive.

### Consequences Persist

Important actions should affect:

- Trust
- Relationships
- Availability of help
- Community morale
- Future conversations
- Later choices
- Character survival
- Rumours and radio reports
- Access to places or information

Not every decision needs a dramatic consequence, but significant decisions should not be forgotten.

### Mystery Without Constant Exposition

The world should contain unanswered questions and conflicting accounts.

Mystery may be delivered through:

- Radio broadcasts
- Rumours
- Environmental clues
- Survivor testimony
- Abandoned locations
- Contradictory evidence
- Recurring symbols or events

The game should not explain everything immediately.

## Inspirations

Creative inspiration includes:

- State of Decay: community survival, expeditions, resource pressure
- The Walking Dead television series: human conflict and relationships
- The Walking Dead by Telltale: character writing and consequential choices
- From: mystery, isolation, and uncertain rules
- Project Zomboid: survival tension and systemic danger
- Stardew Valley: readable top-down presentation and memorable locations

These references must not be copied.

The project needs original:

- Characters
- Plot
- Setting details
- Dialogue
- Factions
- Mechanics
- Visual assets
- Terminology
- Mysteries
- Locations

## Intended Structure

The current preferred structure is:

- A mostly handcrafted world
- A compact county rather than an enormous map
- A safehouse or community hub
- Daily expeditions
- Looting and exploration
- Zombie encounters
- Friendly and hostile survivor interactions
- Handcrafted character situations
- Some simulation-driven events
- A light central story or mystery
- Freedom to delay the main story while surviving

This remains provisional and must be validated through prototypes.

## Potential Core Loop

1. Begin the day at the safehouse.
2. Review supplies, injuries, rumours, and survivor needs.
3. Decide where to go and what risk to take.
4. Explore, loot, avoid or confront zombies.
5. Encounter survivors or changing situations.
6. Make decisions.
7. Return before conditions become too dangerous.
8. Allocate resources and deal with consequences.
9. Advance time.
10. Begin the next day in a changed world.

## Initial Scope

The first prototype should contain:

- One player
- One small test environment
- One searchable container
- One medicine item
- One survivor
- One meaningful choice
- One trust value
- One structured memory
- Save and load

No large world should be built before this interaction is functional.

## Explicit Non-Goals for Early Development

Do not prioritise:

- Multiplayer
- Vehicles
- Huge maps
- Complex base construction
- Dozens of weapons
- Dozens of zombie variants
- Infinite procedural generation
- Hundreds of quests
- Online language-model dialogue
- Photorealistic graphics
- Console or mobile releases

## Visual Direction

The intended style is top-down 2D pixel art or pixel-inspired art.

The visual goal is not retro graphics for their own sake.

The goal is:

- Readability
- Consistency
- Atmosphere
- Strong location identity
- Expressive portraits
- Effective lighting
- Weather
- Day and night
- Good animation feedback

The Compatibility renderer is currently selected to support the development laptop and a broad range of player hardware.

## Open Questions

These decisions remain unresolved:

- Exact world size
- Exact combat style
- Safehouse structure
- Permanent death rules
- Number of controllable survivors
- Whether the player controls one fixed protagonist
- How the central mystery progresses
- How much content is authored versus simulated
- Whether the game has a final ending
- Exact pixel resolution and tile size
- Final title
